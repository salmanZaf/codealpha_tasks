🎣 Phishing Awareness Training

Don't Take the Bait: Protecting Yourself and Your Data

🧠 1. The Attackers' Playbook

Phishing isn't just about bad code; it's a psychological attack known as Social Engineering. Attackers manipulate human emotions to bypass security.

  • Urgency & Fear: "Your account will be suspended in 24 hours if you do not verify your details."
  • Curiosity: "Click here to see the photos from last weekend!"
  • Greed: "You've won a gift card! Claim your prize now."
  • Authority: An email pretending to be the CEO, University Administration, or IT Support demanding immediate action.

📧 2. Recognizing Phishing Emails

Attackers are getting smarter, but they still leave clues. Always check for these red flags before clicking:

  • Mismatched Sender Address: The display name might say "Support," but the actual email address is support-ticket@gmail.com instead of @thecompany.com.
  • Generic Greetings: Using "Dear Customer" or "Dear User" instead of your actual name.
  • Suspicious Links: Hover over (but don't click!) any link to see the actual destination URL.
  • Unexpected Attachments: Invoices or receipts for things you didn't buy, often in .zip, .exe, or macro-enabled .doc formats.
  • Poor Spelling & Grammar: While improving with AI tools, many phishing emails still contain awkward phrasing.

🌐 3. Spotting Fake Websites

If you are redirected to a website, verify its authenticity before entering any credentials:

  • Check the URL Carefully: Look for slight typos (e.g., www.paypa1.com instead of www.paypal.com, or github-login.com instead of github.com).
  • HTTPS is Not Enough: A padlock icon (HTTPS) just means the connection is encrypted; it does not mean the site is legitimate. Attackers use HTTPS too.
  • Examine the Design: Look for low-resolution logos, broken links on the homepage, or missing "Contact Us" and "Privacy Policy" footers.

🚨 4. Real-World Scenarios

Scenario 1: The E-Commerce Store Panic

The Bait: You receive an email stating your domain registrar account requires immediate payment verification, or your online store will be taken offline within hours.

The Catch: The link directs you to a fake login page designed to steal your credentials, giving the attacker access to your business domain or storefront.

Scenario 2: The Cryptocurrency "Withdrawal Alert"

The Bait: An urgent text or email from a cryptocurrency exchange claiming a massive withdrawal of USDT has been initiated from your wallet. It provides a link to "Cancel Transaction."

The Catch: The link goes to a spoofed exchange site. Entering your login and 2FA code hands your wallet keys directly to the attacker.

Scenario 3: The Freelance Client

The Bait: A potential client messages you on a freelance platform and asks you to review a project brief, sending a .rar or .zip file.

The Catch: The file contains malware designed to hijack your browser session tokens.

🛡️ 5. Best Practices & Tips

  • Verify Independently: If you get a suspicious email from your bank or university, do not click the link. Open your browser, type the official URL yourself, and log in to check your account.
  • Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): Always use an authenticator app or hardware key. If an attacker steals your password, they still can't get in without the second factor.
  • Report, Don't Reply: Use your email provider's "Report Phishing" button. Never reply to the attacker.
  • Keep Software Updated: Ensure your operating system, browser, and antivirus are up to date to patch security vulnerabilities.
